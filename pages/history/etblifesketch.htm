Elizabeth Taylor Boyes, 1819-1909
By Emily M. Burningham, 18 May 1979

Elizabeth Taylor was born in Hale, Westmoreland, England, on September 4th, 1819. Her father, James Taylor was the son of Edward Taylor and Elizabeth Saul of Haversham, Westmoreland, England. Her mother, Agnes Taylor, was the daughter of John Taylor and Agnes Whittington of Thorn, Westmoreland, England. She was the sixth of ten children, the second child in the family being John Taylor who later became the President of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints.

Elizabeth came to Canada with her parents in 1828 when she was nine years of age. She married William Arrowsmith when she was twenty-one years old in 1840. He was a miller by trade and some years later was drowned in his own mill race.

Her first child, a son whom they named John after her older brother, was born December 25, 1841, in Millersburg, Mercer County, Iowa.

She heard the gospel message and accepted it as the truth and had an urgent desire to join the body of the saints. Her husband could not see the truth of the gospel and he tried to prevent her from leaving as he loved her very dearly. However, Elizabeth felt that no sacrifice was too great to make for the gospel and even though her husband watched her rather closely she finally succeeded in leaving with her two children. Her second child, a daughter whom she named Hannah had been born in Augusta, Iowa, on the 19th of September, 1844. Hannah was taken ill and died November 4, 1846, just three months before another son, James, was born on February 2, 1847, in Florence, Nebraska. This child lived only until the 8th of May when he too died at the place of his birth.

Elizabeth Taylor left Winter Quarters with the Parley P. Pratt Company along with her parents and her brother, President John Taylor, and his family. She married George Boyes on the trip westward and in spite of all the trials and hardships she had endured she often remarked that she was as happy as a lark feeling that she was doing the right thing. George Boyes was a widower and a father of six children, some of whom were grown. They arrived in Salt Lake Valley on the 19th of September, 1847.

For two years they lived in the fort and then built a home on the property allotted to George Boyes on the northwest corner of 1st West and 2nd South. George purchased a forty acre farm in Big Cottonwood district from a man named John Halladay. Here he built a small adobe house which was later used as a granary after a larger home was erected.

Elizabeth did not want to leave the city and go on a farm. Her health was not very good and her parents were near her as well as her friends. Her first child after her marriage to George Boyes was born in Salt Lake on August 27, 1848, a little girl called Agnes, but she died in infancy. On May 12, 1850 she gave birth to twins, a boy Edward who died and Mary who lived to become the mother of eleven children.

One day as Elizabeth sat by her window, her son John, now a lad of about nine years was singing "Come, Come Ye Saints." When he came to the words "how can I hope to gain a great reward, if I now shun the fight," they struck Elizabeth with great force and she thought, "How can I hope to gain a great reward if I do not go with my husband and make a home where he desires to be."

When she told him she would go with him to the farm, he was very pleased and as long as he lived he was kind and gentle with her. He would quit his work any time to go find help for her when she was not strong enough to do her own work.

On February 26, 1852, she gave birth to a daughter Elizabeth in Big Cottonwood. This daughter lived to be eighty-six years of age and became the mother of 13 children.

Her fifth child by this marriage, Sarah Leonora, was born in the Salt Lake home November 28, 1853. Two of her children passed away in early childhood, but seven were reared to maturity.

On October 25, 1855, a daughter, Rachel, was born but only lived four and one-half years.

George and Elizabeth were prospering and had built a larger adobe home in which they were living when the Johnston Army scare came. Elizabeth was expecting another baby but never-the-less she was forced to leave her home and seek a safer habitation. George dug a pit and such household effects as they could not take with them were buried.

On March 23, 1858, President Brigham Young attended a meeting in Big Cottonwood. He advised the saints to go to Beaver Valley to begin homes again, but most of them got no further than the bottoms north of the Provo R iver. The larger part of the Saints returned to their homes in the Salt Lake Valley in July of 1858, but because of Elizabeth 's condition their family did not return until September. To their great surprise they found on the farm a volunteer stand of grain ready to harvest and over one thousand bushels were garnered. The granary was filled to overflowing and the boys' room in the new house was also filled.

Elizabeth 's baby, a little girl whom they named Ann, was born October 28, 1858. She became the mother of 11 children.

Her next child, George, was born June 8, 1860 and died June 11, 1860, Joseph, her last child was born January 3, 1862. He had 12 children.

In 1867 grasshoppers destroyed the crops. Elizabeth told of how they came the night before the 24th of July and ate everything green in their path, but in spite of them the celebration planned for the holiday was carried out. For the five years following, the crops were more or less destroyed by these pests. Regardless of this condition in Elizabeth 's home they were never without flour.

Even though Elizabeth was far from being a robust woman, she took her place in the community and did her part in carrying on the work of the Lord. When the Relief Society was organized in Big Cottonwood on May 3, 1868, she was sustained as treasurer. June 15, 1870, she was made president of the Relief Society.

On August 1, 1874, her husband passed away leaving her a widow at 55 years of age. Her youngest child was twelve and on him fell the responsibility of carrying on the farm under his mother's capable direction. She owned a home in the city leased to her by her parents located on the site where the Inter-Urban Railway station later stood and by adding four rooms to this she rented the place for over $100.00 a month. She liked to visit the city, and as long as she was able to travel, her son took her whenever she desired to go. They tell that always on the trip home they had a pound of hardtack which was usually consumed by the time the ten mile ride was ended. She never lost her liking for hardtack and peppermints and the grandchildren knew she could always be counted on to supply one or two from her big pockets most anytime.

In 1887 the Relief Society was reorganized and Elizabeth retired from the presidency and acted as first counselor until the end of 1900.

Many tasks fell to the lot of pioneer women in the home. Flax was raised on Elizabeth 's farm and thread was spun from it. Elizabeth 's daughters Elizabeth and Sarah Leonora did most of that spinning. The girls made their own dresses and wore them until the skirt was thin at the back, when back was turned to front and the dress used as a school dress, and a new Sunday one made.

Elizabeth was a splendid housekeeper and even though she directed much of the household activity from her couch, it was carried out to her liking. The children each had a nail for their clothes and in the evening before they retired their clothes were hung on the nails with the shoes, heel touching the wall under them. Before her daughters of hired help could sit down to a meal, the stove must be in order as well as the work table and kitchen areas.

Some of Elizabeth's dresses were made of heavily brocaded silk and were so stiff that the full skirts could almost stand alone. She had quilted petticoats and a big pocket which was tied around the waist and hung under the skirt. As long as she lived, she wore a white net scarf around her neck and a white apron which usually had deep lace on the bottom. She liked her children to have nice things and loved to get them pieces of jewelry to wear. She was very hospitable and entertained the authorities of the church and their wives a great deal when they came to Big Cottonwood. Never a friend came in, that she did not send one of the children out to get a pan of chips, and refreshments were served them.

She enjoyed the brass band which the community boasted, and we would dance down the path, when they stopped to play in front of the house, and toss a dollar to them.

With her own hands she helped plant many fruit and shade trees, a number of which are still standing and bearing today.

In her patriarchal blessing given December 12, 1847, by John Smith, she was told that she should have sons and daughters excelled by none in Israel . Also that she should heal the sick by the prayer of faith when no elder was present. Among her posterity are stake authorities, ward authorities, many missionaries, educators, musicians, and many fine and honorable people.

Once, when she was quite poorly, Sisters Eliza R. Snow, Zina Young, and Elizabeth Howard came to wash and anoint her, and during the ceremony Sister Snow spoke in tongues and interpreted the same.

During her life time she did a great deal of temple work both in the Logan Temple and after its completion in the Salt Lake Temple .

She loved her children and grandchildren very dearly and often in her very old age when asked if the children annoyed her she would say, "Oh, no, their noise is music to my ears." Almost as long as she lived Christmas was an outstanding event as her posterity gathered at her home. She lived to be almost ninety, passing away just a few days before her birthday. She died August 31, 1909. She left six children, about fifty grandchildren and many great-grandchildren to carry on her memory and bring honor to her name.
