Autobiography of Lavina Moses Jacobs
Written sometime around 1998 by Lavina Jacobs

My parents Jesse Tilton Moses, Jr., son of Jesse Tilton Moses Sr., and Phoebe Arabell Woodruff and Lavina Harper, daughter of Charles Alfred Harper Jr. and Mary Boyes were married September 23, 1903, in the Salt Lake Temple. I was born June 30, 1910 in Smithfield Cache County. Utah, the fourth of nine children. My brothers and sisters were Mary, Jesse Harper, Arabell, Vilate, Charles, Erma, who died in infancy, Fern and Leone. My parents were both born and raised in Big Cottonwood, which is now part of Salt Lake City. My parents and grandparents moved to Smithfield and purchased a mice farm. My father built a neat little yellow brick house near his fathers home and I was born in that house.

One of my earliest recollections was a large raspberry patch with a path through the middle I made many trips over that path to my grandparents place. When the berries were ripe, it was mice to pick and eat them as I walked along the path.

My grandparents kept bees and in the fall when the honey was gathered Grandma made a huge batch of honey candy. We children were all given a piece to stretch. It was fun living close to my grandparents. We had lots of fun times.

When I was five, my father went to Nevada to homestead some land. This was not a good move as water was scarce and jackrabbits were not. They ate the grain and other crops as soon as they began to show in the spring. The three years that we lived in Nevada were not happy ones. My mother had a baby daughter who lived only three weeks. This was a great sorrow to our family. My brother Charles was also born there. The last winter we were there, we children all had scarlet fever and were very sick. My sister Mary almost died, but through our faith and prayers she recovered.

In the spring of 1917, we moved to Lost River, Idaho where my father bought a farm. I had started school in Nevada so I was in the second grade when we moved to Idaho. I enjoyed the five years we lived in Lost River, though we lived on a farm and there was much work to do. I helped herd the cows, weed the garden, and other chores that I was old enough to help with. A very important event took place in my life. I was baptized in the Lost River July 6, 1918, and confirmed the next day at church. I was proud to be a member of the church.

We had neighbors who had children our age so we had fun playing together. There was a canal close by where we could swim and in the summer evening we would play outside games. Sometimes mother and dad played ball with us which we thought was great.

Thanksgiving, Christmas and birthdays were always special. Mother was an excellent cook and always fixed such mice dinners. These are all such special memories for me.

I enjoyed my years in elementary school. There were four of us girls who were very close friends and we had fun taking part in plays and programs we had for holidays and special occasions.

In 1918, the flu epidemic hit our little community. Our family was all sick at the same time. My uncle lived close by, and he looked after us. He did the chores and got things at the store for us. I don't know what we would have done without him.

My sister and I were very sick and were down for three weeks, but the rest of the family was up and about in a few days.

World War I was taking place at this time and my father was called to register for the draft. He came home from town with a big smile on his face and said the war had ended. You can imagine how happy we were.

When my sister Mary graduated from eighth grade she went to Smithfield and stayed with our grandparents and went to high school. Then when my older brother was ready for high school, we moved to Blackfoot where we would be close to high school for the rest of us. I was in seventh grade so I went to junior high. I did quite well in all my classes except math, which was always hard for me, but I made it. During my eighth grade year I was neither absent nor tardy so I received a certificate at graduation. Now I was ready for high school.

My first three years went along quite smoothly but the fall of my senior year my brother Charles and my mother both had small pox and were very sick. Of course we were quarantined so I missed several weeks of school. Then my father rented a large farm in the little town of Pingree so we moved out there about seventeen miles from Blackfoot. This caused me to miss a year of school. There was a two-year high school in Pingree so I went to a half year and picked up two credits, which helped in my senior year in Blackfoot. The next winter my sister Vilate and I rented a room in a friend’s home and I finished high school and graduated in May 1930.

The next two years I stayed at home and helped my father and mother. The great depression had begun and it was almost impossible for girls to find work. My older brother was married, also my sister Mary. My sister Arabell was working as a secretary in the Logan Temple. The fall of 1932 a lady in Blackfoot called and wanted me to come and work for her. She was a partial invalid and needed help in her home. I was glad for this opportunity to earn some money. I worked for the Howell family for three years. Then I felt a desire to fill a mission and began to make preparations to go.

I was happy and excited when I received my call to the Southern States. I spent ten days in the mission home in Salt Lake City, which was a great experience. We left Salt Lake at 10:00 p.m. December 3, 1936 on the D & R railroad and arrived in Atlanta, Georgia December 6. That evening we attended a Missionary meeting and were assigned our companions. My companion was Sister Nelda Bills and she was a jewel. I began my mission in Savannah, Georgia. Sister Bills was an excellent missionary and helped me get a good start. She and I have kept in touch over the years. I spent three months in Savannah, nine months in Macon, Georgia, and six months in Montgomery, Alabama.

I enjoyed my mission very much and a very important event happened to me. I met my future husband, Elder Augustus Twitchell in the little town of Mognolia, Alabama where we had gone to a branch conference. He came from the small community of Antimony, Utah.

After we came home we kept in touch and it wasn’t long until we knew we were in love. After a short courtship we were married in the Logan Temple December 23, 1940. We spent Christmas at my parent’s home in Riverside, a farming community five miles out of Blackfoot. They had bought a farm and built a home on it. On New Years day we loaded my things in our car and drove to Antimony. I began to wonder what I had got myself into but I soon got acquainted with his family. There were six boys and two girls. Gus was the oldest, then Howel, Ralph, Zelma, Arthur, Melba, Clarence, and Ephraim. Gus s father, William Henry Twitchell, died March 24, 1938, so I never met him but some day I will. Gus’ mother, Nancy Ellen Openshaw, was a very nice person. She was so kind and helpful to me and I loved her dearly. We lived at the Center Creek ranch for a while as the boys were sorting potatoes and doing other work.

One day a friend came to see Gus and ask if he would like to rent his farm. We quickly took his offer, as we needed a place to live. The farm was located on the Antimony bench and we were soon moved into the small home and were happy. We worked hard to get the land ready for spring planting. We planted a garden that produced a lot of nice vegetables and in late summer fruit peddlers came to our town so I was able to can what fruit we needed.

In September 1941 Mr. Woodard, the man who owned the farm, died of complications following surgery. We rented the farm from his wife for a year and then bought it. With hard work and good management we raised good crops and soon had it paid for. With eighty acres of good farmland and a cattle permit, we made a good living. I always raised a garden and canned lots of fruit, vegetables and meat so my fruit room was always full. Over the years I cooked many meals for hired help. In 1941 World War II started. Three of Gus’ brothers were drafted into the Army. Arthur, Clarence and Ephraim were all in training in Camp Roberts, California at the same time. Arthur was sent to France, where he was killed in action. This, was a great sorrow to the family. Clarence went to the South Pacific and Ephraim to Italy. They both returned home. We were all happy when the war ended.

Our first child, Dennis, was born February 8, 1942. Our second son David was born February 20, 1944. Our third son Terry came to us April 23, 1947. These three boys were healthy, happy children and we loved and enjoyed them. They kept us busy taking care of them. September 13, 1948 we were blessed with a sweet little girl, Lois Marie but how sad we were when we learned that she was born with a heart problem. We took her to a specialist but they could do nothing for here. She died January 31, 1949. Another darling little girl, Rhonda Jean was born January 27, 1952. She was a healthy, beautiful baby and so full of fun. Everyone loved her. Our joy soon turned to sorrow as she took ill on October 30 and died November 2, 1952. I guess some of these special spirits came to earth to get a body but if we are worthy we will be with them again some day. We enjoyed our boys and did all we could to make a good home for them.

My mother died June 25, 1956 at age 77. She was born in 1879. We enjoyed camping or just going on a picnic on the mountain. Each summer we came to Idaho to visit my family. We also took our boys to the state and national parks and other interesting places. As they grew older, they were a great help to us on the farm, especially in the hay and potato harvest. Most of the families in our town were active members of the church and in a small ward everyone is kept busy. I taught classes in Primary, Sunday school, M.I.A. and Relief Society. I was a counselor in the Primary presidency for several years and then in Y.W.M.I.A. I was the president of Young Women for several years. In 1950, I was set apart as Relief Society President. I helped on many committees for ward socials and prepared food for dinners and parties. I was busy but enjoyed helping in all the ward activities. Gus also held many positions in the ward. Pie worked in the Sunday School and M.I.A. He was one of the seven presidents of the seventies Quorum.

November 6, 1948, he was ordained a high Priest and set apart as first counselor to Bishop Van Wiley. Then in August of 1953, he was ordained and set apart as Bishop of the Antimony Ward. After his release as Bishop, he was called into the Stake high Council where he served for several years. June 15, 1962 Grandma Twitchell died of cancer. She was a sweet lady and loved by all who knew her.

Our son Dennis was married in September of 1963 and our second son David was called to serve a mission in January 1964 to the Northwestern States. Terry was called to serve in the South California Mission in June 1966.

At this time the US was involved in the Vietnam War. David was drafted so he joined the navy, He was in basic training the same time Terry was finishing his mission in San Diego. California. Terry came home in June 1968, and David had a military leave in August. While he was on leave he and Nedra Hatch were married in the St. George Temple.

After David was released from the navy he and Nedra returned to college in Cedar City. Terry had transferred to Utah State in Logan. Dennis was busy trucking and Gus and I were busy on the farm. Things seemed to be going along nicely when Gus suffered a heart attack in April 1970 and spent several days in the hospital. He was unable to take care of the farm work so David came home to help. In fact, he took over.

At 7 P. M. on July 15, 1970 Gus was sifting in his chair reading the newspaper when he had a massive heart attack and died. It was a great sorrow to all of us but I think it was his time to go. We had a very nice funeral service and buried him in a beautiful hard wood casket. He had many friends who sent cards and flowers. David was a great help to me. He finished the farm work and harvested the hay and grain. He also took over the farm work the next summer. David and Nedra's first son Dale was born September 7, 1970. Dermis and Linda had two boys aged five and three. We loved and enjoyed them but Gus did not live to see our other ten grandchildren. When Terry was attending school in Logan, he met Diane Pettingill. They were married September 18, 1970 in the Salt Lake temple. I am sure that Gus was there with us at the wedding.

The winter of 70 and 71 was long and lonesome for me. That summer I went on a church history tour. We went to Radio City in New York. We saw the Joseph Smith home and went to the scared Grove, which is a spiritual experience. We enjoyed our visit to Nauvoo and the Hill Cumorah pageant. This experience was good for me and I felt better when I returned home.

My father passed away June 15, 1971 in St. George, at age 93. He was buried in the Thomas Riverside Cemetery in Idaho.

David took care of the farm in 1971. In the spring of 1972, we sold the place to Charlie Brindly and Van Wiley. It had been our home for thirty years and we hated to part with it but we felt it was the best thing to do. I was glad to be free of the responsibility.

Dennis was busy driving truck. David went to Idaho to help my brother Charles on his farm and Terry had finished college and was working for the Forest service. My sister Mary and I went to Parma in June to attend our family reunion. At this time I was living in Neil Ipson’s house in town. It was a nice little home and I enjoyed living in it. I kept the lawn mowed and watered Terry and Diane were in California working for the Forest Service so I decided to spend Thanksgiving with them. I enjoyed my visit with them. They came to Utah for Christmas and spent some time with me as well as with Diane’s family in Kaysville. So the winter of ‘72 passed.

In June of 1973, I went on a Temple tour to California and St. George. Then in July I went on a tour to Canada to British Columbia and Alberta. We saw lots of beautiful scenery and enjoyed the tour. The group was not LDS so we did not go to the Cardston Temple, but we did go and see it.

Immediately after I returned from my trip to Canada I drove to Richfield and found an apartment. I spent two days cleaning then moved the first of August. David and Nedra were visiting in Koosharem so they helped me move. Dennis and Linda were living in Richfield and I wanted to be near them. The first Sunday that I went to church I met Clifton Rees. He was just finishing his 2nd term as mayor of Richfield. We became good friends and enjoyed each other for a year. We were married September 14, 1974. In October we received our call to the Canada Vancouver Mission. We arrived in Vancouver January 25, 1975. Our first assignment was Prince Rupert. We were there for six months. We were in Victoria six months then Osoyoos six months. We did not have much success teaching or baptizing here. There were hard feelings in the branch and it was about to fall apart. The branch was reorganized and Cliff was set apart as a counselor and by the time we left in July they had it going quite smoothly. I helped in the Relief Society. I guess that was our purpose for being sent to this area. We enjoyed our mission and felt we had accomplished some good in each area. We left the mission field July 15, 1976. We had a nice trip home and received a nice welcome We arrived in Salt Lake in a very heavy rainstorm. We gave our report in our ward and were asked to speak in several wards in the stake.

We had not been home too long when Cliff began having health problems. He had two heart bypasses and died of congestive heart failure November 2, 1977. There I was alone and lonesome again. I spent Thanksgiving in Idaho with my family and Christmas in Richfield with Dennis and Linda. I had always wanted to see the Rose Parade so I made reservations with the James Tour Co. to go to Pasadena for New Years to see the parade. Strange things happen when you least expect them. Chariton Jacobs from Mt. Pleasant, Utah was on that tour. I sat in the seat in front of him and we talked and got acquainted. We stayed in Las Vegas that night and the next day we sat together on the bus. We spent much of our time together on the tour and enjoyed each other s company. When we arrived home, we kept in touch and soon decided that we wanted to get married. We were married April 12, 1978 in the Manti Temple. He had a nice home in Mt. Pleasant and we spent 15½ happy years in it.

Chariton was named after his grandfather who was born on the banks of the Chariton River soon after the saints were driven out of Nauvoo. His family and friends called him Charry. He was born and raised in Mt. Pleasant and attended Snow College and BYU. He worked with his father as a mortician for many years and then by himself after his father died. He retired and sold his business soon after we were married. We went to Hawaii on our honeymoon and had a great time.

We belonged to the fourth ward and my first church assignment was Primary secretary. I was a visiting teacher for 15½ years. I was called to be Compassionate Service Leader for three years. I was invited to join the Fine Arts Club, which I enjoyed. It was such a nice group of ladies and I loved all of them. There were special occasions when we invited our husbands.

In 1979, Charry and I were called to the Washington Spokane Mission. We spent nine months in Grand Coolee, three months in Republic, and six months in Quincy. We had the privilege of going to the dedication of the Seattle Temple. We enjoyed our eighteen months in the mission field. We enjoyed working with the Elders and the other couples. We worked with less active members and had good success in helping them become active again. We also encouraged parents who had children over eight years old to have them baptized. We enjoyed our mission and felt we helped in some areas. After we returned home, we decided to have a carport built. This took some time and a lot of work but it was worth it. We surely enjoyed having it. Mt. Pleasant City installed a sewer system so of course we hooked up to that. On June 24, Charry and I were set apart as ordinance workers in the Provo Temple. We worked there for five years and enjoyed every day of it. Charry said it was the highlight of his life. He enjoyed genealogy and temple work. We also went to the Manti Temple quite often. Charry and I had a very good marriage. We had the same interests and enjoyed doing things together. We went on many church history and temple tours. We also went to see other places of interest. In fact we covered the whole United States and much of Canada. We visited twenty temples. Charry had a very nice family and I was accepted and made to feel like one of them. He had two daughters and one son. He also had two brothers and a sister. We had family reunions and spent many holidays and special occasions together. Our home was a gathering place and we often had a house full but they were always welcome. On the Sunday of October conference 1993, Charry’s family all came to Mt. Pleasant. We had a picnic lunch at the picnic area up by the power plant during the noon break between conference sessions. We had such a nice time together and Charry enjoyed it so much. After the afternoon reunion, Annette took us for a ride around the Mt. Nebo scenic loop. This was our last family gathering. At 10 P.M. Friday, October 8, Charry had a very bad stroke. We took him to the hospital in Provo where he passed away October 13, 1993. He was in the hospital on his 81 birthday, October 10. Karen, Annette, Dennis and I were with him when he died. We had a very nice funeral service for him Saturday, October 16. He was buried in the Mt. Pleasant cemetery next to his wife Fern McIntosh. I miss him very much. He was a very kind, thoughtful man. After the funeral my family all came to my home to visit and decide what was best for me to do. I wanted to move to Idaho to be near David and Terry. Dennis lived in Salt Lake, but I did not want to live there. Terry and Diane knew of a nice little home in Shelley that was for sale so we decided that I should buy the house and live in Shelley. They went home and took care of all the business of buying the house for me. I am so thankful for all they did for me. A month after Charry died my brother Harper, who lived in Parma, Idaho, passed away. My sister Mary and I planned to go on the bus but Dennis helped us with the plane tickets so we flew. We were happy to see many family members whom we had not seen for some time.

When we returned, I stayed in Salt Lake until after Thanksgiving, which we enjoyed at Mike and Sharon's s home. The next day Dennis took me back to Mt. Pleasant. The business on the house in Shelley was progressing and I had a lot of packing to do so December was a busy month for me. I had my living room filled with boxes and bags. I was invited to Salt Lake for Christmas and I was getting ready to move so I did not make any preparations at home. Gary came December 23 and took me to Salt Lake. The day after Christmas, Dennis rented a big truck. David, Terry, Jeff and a friend came to help me load the truck. Terry drove my car back to Idaho. I rode with David in the U. Haul and his friend drove his pick up. Annette and Dean and their boys and Karen came to say goodbye. I said a tearful goodbye to my home in Mt. Pleasant. I had lived there 15½ years and enjoyed it very much. I arrived in Shelley and saw my new home for the first time. It was a nice little house and I really liked it. I stayed with Terry and Diane a few days until we got the truck unloaded and I could start keeping house.

I moved into my house January 3, 1994 and enjoyed living there until the fall of that year. A very nice senior citizen complex had been completed on West Pine street. The apartments were very nice so I decided to apply for one. Number 12 was still available so I moved into it December 13, 1994. I had no trouble selling the house so things worked very nicely for me. I see that I was blessed in being able to make the move. It is a perfect situation for me. I do not have to worry about doing yard work or making house payments. The other tenants are very nice and we have some fun times together. We have dinners in the community room and on summer evening we sit out and enjoy visiting. The complex is located in the Eighth Ward but my bishop gave me permission to stay in the Fourth Ward since that is the ward Terry and Diane belong to. I plan to live here the rest of my life but I cannot see into the future.

I want my family to know how much I love them and all they have done for me and are still doing. Terry and Diane are taking good care of me and it is a comfort to know they are close by and willing to help if I need it. I still enjoy good health and am able to take care of myself.

I am thankful for the many blessings I have received during my life. I am thankful for my membership in the church and the feeling of security I have to know that I belong to the true church. I know the Lord loves and watches over us. I love the gospel and have a strong testimony.
